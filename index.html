4/10 on assessment · Put-call parity · Greeks · BSM · VIOP TRY hedge

Assessment Finding
4/10 — foundational concepts are unclear. These directly affect how you hedge your BIST and S&P 500 positions using VIOP and SPX puts. This is not an advanced topic — it is core portfolio management.
You don't need to be an options trader. You need derivatives to: (1) hedge S&P 500 holdings during US downturns with index puts; (2) use VIOP USD/TRY futures to hedge TRY depreciation on BIST holdings; (3) understand structured products your bank sells; (4) read VIX and BIST implied volatility as market sentiment signals. 🇹🇷 VIOP — Your Practical TRY Hedge: VIOP (Vadeli İşlem ve Opsiyon Piyasası) on Borsa Istanbul offers USD/TRY and EUR/TRY futures, BIST-30 index futures, and single-stock options. If you hold 1M TRY in BIST stocks and fear TRY depreciation, buying USD/TRY futures locks in your exchange rate. Understanding futures pricing (cost-of-carry model) lets you evaluate whether the hedge cost is fair before placing any trade.

Beta, CAPM & Cost of Capital
Regression Beta · Bottom-Up Beta · EM Risk Premium · WACC for Turkish Stocks
Wks 1–2 · ~12 hrs Priority Gap ▼
Assessment Finding — 100-Question Test
Equity Analysis 6/10 — Beta and CAPM mechanics were directly tested. The likely gap: understanding how β = 1.5 flows through to an expected return via the SML, why regression beta is unreliable for thinly-traded BIST stocks (staleness bias), and how to construct bottom-up beta using Damodaran's sector data. This module addresses all three.
Why You Missed It & Why It Matters
Beta is defined as β = Cov(Rᵢ, Rₘ) / Var(Rₘ) — the OLS slope of a stock's excess return regressed on the market's excess return. You know this math. The miss was likely on the interpretation: what β = 1.5 means for expected return via the Security Market Line, or how to compute a bottom-up beta for a BIST company with thinly-traded shares where regression beta is noisy and unreliable. For Turkish investors, understanding the full chain — risk-free rate → equity risk premium → country risk premium → beta → cost of equity → WACC — is essential for any DCF valuation.
🔬 Your Existing Skill Maps Here: Beta estimation is OLS with one regressor. You already know how to run this, interpret residuals, and understand confidence intervals. The financial layer to add: (1) why monthly data over 5 years is convention, (2) what the intercept (Jensen's alpha) means, (3) why for BIST stocks you should prefer bottom-up beta constructed from industry peers rather than your stock's thin trading history.
🇹🇷 BIST-Specific Problem: Many BIST stocks trade infrequently. Regression beta calculated from daily/weekly BIST data suffers from staleness bias — the "Scholes-Williams" or "Dimson" corrections exist for this. Damodaran's bottom-up approach (sector beta → un-lever → re-lever for your company's capital structure) bypasses this and is more reliable. His emerging market beta datasets include Turkey sector betas updated annually.

Why This Matters
The mechanics of DCF — discounting future cash flows — is just NPV from your engineering background. A DCF for a Turkish company must embed this via a higher discount rate — which mechanically lowers the fair value. Many retail BIST investors ignore this and wonder why their "cheap" stocks stay cheap. Your engineering intuition about discount rates should make this click immediately.

Why This Matters
Factor investing bridges your statistics background to portfolio management. A factor is just a systematic variable that explains cross-sectional return differences across stocks — exactly the kind of regression/ML problem you already work with. The Fama-French 5-factor model (Market, Size, Value, Profitability, Investment) is a multiple regression of stock returns on portfolio sorts. Your ML background makes the critical extension — ML for alpha research and cross-validation to avoid overfitting — directly applicable.
🔬 Critical Connection — "Factor Zoo": Harvey & Liu (2019) documented 400+ published factors. Your statistics training immediately raises the right question: with this many variables tested against the same data, most discoveries are false positives. The Bonferroni correction and familywise error rates apply directly. This is the most important methodological insight in modern empirical finance — and you already have the tools to understand it rigorously.

Targeted Gap Topics
SectionSpecific GapKey Insight to Internalise
Fixed Income
Z-spread vs OAS vs YTM spread
Z-spread = constant bp added to every node of the zero coupon curve. OAS = Z-spread minus the option value embedded in the bond (callable bonds).
Price = Σ CFₜ / (zₜ + Z)ᵗ for all t. Unlike YTM spread (flat curve assumption), Z-spread respects the full term structure.
Fixed Income
Convexity and callable bond negative convexity
Positive convexity: bond gains more than it loses for equal yield moves. Callable bond: issuer calls when rates fall (capping price upside) → negative convexity above par.
ΔP/P ≈ −ModDur × Δy + ½ × Convexity × (Δy)². Plot price-yield for straight vs callable bond — see the cap clearly.
Inv. Basics
Why EV/EBITDA dominates P/E for cross-market comparison
EV/EBITDA sits above capital structure (debt), tax treatment, and D&A policy — all of which differ enormously between Turkey and US companies.
A Turkish company with high leverage, 22% corporate tax, and IFRS D&A has a distorted P/E that EV/EBITDA corrects for.
Inv. Basics
DDM Gordon Growth instability & accruals quality
As g → r, P → ∞. This is why stable-growth DDM only applies to mature, slow-growth firms. Accruals = net income − CFO; large positive accruals signal potential earnings manipulation.
For BIST banks: accruals are critical because loan loss provisions are discretionary — a key earnings quality red flag.

Understanding futures pricing (cost-of-carry model) lets you evaluate whether the hedge cost is fair.
